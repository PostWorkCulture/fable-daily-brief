1

OpenAI lets everyone use its newest chatbot brain

OpenAI (the company behind ChatGPT) had been told by the US government to only let a few big organisations use its newest AI model while officials checked it was safe from being used for cyberattacks. That check finished early, so now anyone can use it — and one version is just as smart as their best model but half the price.

2

Claude gets a big free upgrade

Anthropic (who makes Claude) released Claude Sonnet 5, a new version that’s much better at planning tasks, writing code and reasoning through problems — and it’s now the version most free and Pro users get by default.

3

Chinese AI models are taking over almost half the market

“Open source” means the AI is free for anyone to download and build on, instead of locked away by one company. Chinese open-source AI models are now used for nearly half of all business AI traffic in America, mostly because they’re 60–90% cheaper to run than the big US ones.

4

A free Chinese AI model beats the big paid ones at coding

A Chinese company called Z.ai released a huge open-source (free for anyone to use) AI model that beat some of the best paid models at coding tests, and it was trained on Chinese-made computer chips instead of the usual American ones.

5

DeepSeek is building its own AI computer chip

DeepSeek, a well-known Chinese AI company, is reportedly designing its own special computer chip to run its AI, so it depends less on American chip-makers like Nvidia, which are restricted from selling their best chips to China.

6

The first AI hacking attack with no human involved

Security researchers found the first “ransomware” attack (where hackers lock your files and demand payment) that was run entirely by an AI “agent” — meaning an AI that can act on its own — with no person guiding it step by step. It broke in, found weaknesses and locked over a thousand files by itself.

7

More people use AI, but fewer people trust it

Surveys show more people than ever are using AI tools like chatbots every day — but most say they only trust what those tools tell them “sometimes” or “rarely”. Most people also think AI is moving faster than governments can keep up with.

South Korea’s government announced an $880 billion, 10-year plan to build more computer chips, AI data centres and robots — including a goal to go from barely making any humanoid robots to making a fifth of the world’s supply.

Your local council (Kingston) is already using AI at work

Kingston Council, near where you live, now uses an AI tool called “Magic Notes” to help social workers write up notes after meetings. Work that used to take 2 hours now takes about 30 minutes, and it’s reportedly accurate about 96% of the time.

Getting Started with AI

You don’t need any technical background to start understanding AI — and you don’t need to learn to code. The best way in is simply to try a couple of short, free courses and to play with the tools yourself. Go at your own pace; a little curiosity is all it takes.

Free Courses to Try

1

Elements of AI

A free, friendly online course from the University of Helsinki — no maths or programming. Over 2 million people have taken it. Great first step.

2

AI For Everyone (Coursera)

Andrew Ng’s short, non-technical course explaining what AI can and can’t do. You can enrol and watch all the videos for free (audit).

3

Generative AI for Everyone (Coursera)

A plain-English course on tools like ChatGPT — how to write good prompts and use AI in everyday tasks. Free to audit.

Learn by Doing

The fastest way to learn is to use AI for a small real task. Ask claude.ai or chatgpt.com to help draft an email, plan a trip, or explain something you’re curious about. For a fun, visual taste of how AI “learns”, try Teachable Machine — you can train a model to recognise things using just your webcam, no code needed.

A Simple Way to Pace It

Week 1: Start Elements of AI — just the first part. Week 2: Try AI For Everyone and use a chatbot for one real task. Week 3: Watch Generative AI for Everyone and practise writing clearer prompts. Week 4: Play with Teachable Machine and finish whichever course you enjoyed most. That’s a solid, no-pressure introduction in a month.
