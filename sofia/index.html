Getting Started with AI

You don’t need any technical background to start understanding AI — and you don’t need to learn to code. The best way in is simply to try a couple of short, free courses and to play with the tools yourself. Go at your own pace; a little curiosity is all it takes.

Free Courses to Try

1

Elements of AI

A free, friendly online course from the University of Helsinki — no maths or programming. Over 2 million people have taken it. Great first step.

2

AI For Everyone (Coursera)

Andrew Ng’s short, non-technical course explaining what AI can and can’t do. You can enrol and watch all the videos for free (audit).

3

Generative AI for Everyone (Coursera)

A plain-English course on tools like ChatGPT — how to write good prompts and use AI in everyday tasks. Free to audit.

Learn by Doing

The fastest way to learn is to use AI for a small real task. Ask claude.ai or chatgpt.com to help draft an email, plan a trip, or explain something you’re curious about. For a fun, visual taste of how AI “learns”, try Teachable Machine — you can train a model to recognise things using just your webcam, no code needed.

A Simple Way to Pace It

Week 1: Start Elements of AI — just the first part. Week 2: Try AI For Everyone and use a chatbot for one real task. Week 3: Watch Generative AI for Everyone and practise writing clearer prompts. Week 4: Play with Teachable Machine and finish whichever course you enjoyed most. That’s a solid, no-pressure introduction in a month.

1

Anthropic is now earning more than OpenAI

Anthropic (the company that makes Claude) is now bringing in more money than OpenAI (the maker of ChatGPT). It’s a sign of how fast the race between AI companies is changing.

2

The first AI virus that runs on its own

Security experts found the first “ransomware” — a nasty program that locks your files — that an AI ran completely by itself, with no human controlling it. It’s a warning about AI being misused.

3

Five countries agree on safety rules for ‘agent’ AIs

The UK, US, Australia, Canada and New Zealand wrote shared advice for keeping “agentic” AI safe — that means AI that can take actions on its own, not just answer questions.

4

China’s cheap AI is catching up

A Chinese AI called GLM-5.2 is nearly as good as the top US models but much cheaper. People are debating whether China is closing the gap in the AI race.

5

A powerful new free AI called Kimi K2

A Chinese company released Kimi K2, a big AI that’s free for anyone to use and build on (that’s what “open” means). It’s built to carry out tasks, not just chat.

6

Another free Chinese AI: Hunyuan 3.0

Tencent, a huge Chinese tech company, shared a powerful AI that anyone can use for free. Releasing AI openly like this lets others learn from and improve it.

7

DeepSeek’s newest model is out

DeepSeek, another Chinese lab, released the full version of its latest AI (V4) after a sneak-peek earlier this year. It keeps China among the leaders in free AI.

8

Elon Musk’s Grok tops the tests

xAI’s new Grok 4.5 scored top marks on several tests, including a tough one that checks how well an AI can do long coding jobs.

9

A local London council is using AI

Kingston Council (near us) is starting to use AI to work faster and improve services — a sign that even everyday local government is trying the technology.

10

New EU cars must watch if you’re distracted

From 7 July, all brand-new cars in the EU must include AI that watches a driver’s eyes and head to spot when they stop paying attention — a safety feature.
