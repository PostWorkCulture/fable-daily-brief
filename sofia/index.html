Top 10 in AI — Explained Simply

1

Free, downloadable Chinese AI models are now more popular than Western ones

“Open-source” AI means anyone can download and use the model for free, instead of paying a company to access it. Chinese open-source models made up 41% of downloads on Hugging Face (a site where people share AI models) this spring, and the six most-downloaded models right now are all Chinese. One of them, GLM 5.2, performs almost as well as Anthropic’s best model on a tough test — for roughly a fifth of the cost to run.

2

Rival AI companies suddenly agree on how AI should be regulated

“Regulation” means rules set by government. For the first time, the bosses of OpenAI, Anthropic and Google DeepMind have each published detailed plans for AI regulation that turned out to be strikingly similar — and rival CEOs praised each other’s ideas, which is unusual in such a competitive industry.

3

Google’s AI just got a “deep thinking” upgrade

Google released a new version of its AI assistant Gemini with an extra-careful reasoning mode that scores well on tricky science questions. It can also read roughly two million “tokens” (small chunks of text) at once — about the length of several long novels — showing how fast AI keeps improving.

4

OpenAI’s newest model is now built into Microsoft Office

OpenAI’s GPT-5.6 is now the default AI inside Microsoft 365 Copilot (the AI helper built into Word, Outlook and other Office apps) — a sign of how quickly brand-new AI models reach everyday work tools.

5

Anthropic (the company behind Claude) is now the AI industry’s top earner

Anthropic is on track to make around $47 billion this year and is reportedly even profitable — unusual for an AI company, since most lose money. Meanwhile OpenAI is reportedly offering the US government a huge ownership stake, and Google is limiting how much of its AI, Gemini, it lets rival Meta use.

6

A phone-chip company wants to buy an AI chip startup for up to $10bn

Qualcomm, best known for chips inside smartphones, is reportedly in early talks to buy Tenstorrent, a startup that makes AI chips — part of a race by chipmakers to find alternatives to Nvidia, which currently makes most of the chips AI runs on.

7

Google opens a new AI lab for African researchers

The Africa Applied AI Lab gives researchers and entrepreneurs in Africa early access to Google’s AI tools — part of a wider push to make sure the benefits of AI reach beyond the richest countries.

8

The UK is behind on using AI in government — ranked 6th out of 10 countries

A new report scored the UK just 47 out of 100 for how well its public sector uses AI, behind Saudi Arabia, Singapore, India and South Africa. More than half of civil servants say they’ve had no AI training at all.

9

“Shadow AI”: lots of UK civil servants are secretly using AI at work

“Shadow AI” means using AI tools your employer hasn’t officially approved. The same report found that in teams with little official AI support, most enthusiastic AI users log into personal ChatGPT-style accounts for work — and most don’t tell their manager. A new government “AI Fellows” programme aims to close that gap.

10

Kingston Council: still no official AI plan

No standalone AI strategy has been published by Kingston Council this week either — AI adoption is still folded into its wider digital and productivity plans, the same as before.

Getting Started with AI

You don’t need any technical background to start understanding AI — and you don’t need to learn to code. The best way in is simply to try a couple of short, free courses and to play with the tools yourself. Go at your own pace; a little curiosity is all it takes.

Free Courses to Try

1

Elements of AI

A free, friendly online course from the University of Helsinki — no maths or programming. Over 2 million people have taken it. Great first step.

2

AI For Everyone (Coursera)

Andrew Ng’s short, non-technical course explaining what AI can and can’t do. You can enrol and watch all the videos for free (audit).

3

Generative AI for Everyone (Coursera)

A plain-English course on tools like ChatGPT — how to write good prompts and use AI in everyday tasks. Free to audit.

Learn by Doing

The fastest way to learn is to use AI for a small real task. Ask claude.ai or chatgpt.com to help draft an email, plan a trip, or explain something you’re curious about. For a fun, visual taste of how AI “learns”, try Teachable Machine — you can train a model to recognise things using just your webcam, no code needed.

A Simple Way to Pace It

Week 1: Start Elements of AI — just the first part. Week 2: Try AI For Everyone and use a chatbot for one real task. Week 3: Watch Generative AI for Everyone and practise writing clearer prompts. Week 4: Play with Teachable Machine and finish whichever course you enjoyed most. That’s a solid, no-pressure introduction in a month.
